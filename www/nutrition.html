Why carbohydrate cravings are real right now
Estrogen is at its lowest point in your cycle right now. Estrogen directly improves insulin sensitivity. When estrogen drops, your cells become temporarily less responsive to insulin. Your body compensates by craving quick glucose.
This is not weak willpower. It is a measurable metabolic response to low estrogen.
What actually helps: complex carbohydrates paired with protein rather than simple sugar. The protein slows glucose absorption and reduces the insulin spike while still addressing the metabolic demand. Sweet potato with Greek yogurt, oats with eggs, rice with fish.
Source: Mauvais-Jarvis F et al. Journal of Clinical Investigation 2013. Estrogen receptors on muscle and pancreatic beta cells and the estrogen-insulin sensitivity mechanism.
Find relief for
🥊
Cramping
Dysmenorrhea and pelvic pain
🐟
Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines)
Rich in EPA and DHA omega-3 fatty acids. Dietary omega-3s may reduce prostaglandin production, which drives uterine contractions and cramping. Aim for 2 to 3 servings per week, particularly in the week before your period.
Source: Harel Z et al. Am J Obstet Gynecol. 1996;174(4):1335-1338 (n=42 adolescents, omega-3 supplementation significantly reduced dysmenorrhea severity vs placebo)
🍫
Pumpkin seeds and dark chocolate (70%+)
Both are high in magnesium. Magnesium may reduce uterine muscle contractions and inhibit prostaglandin synthesis. Pumpkin seeds provide approximately 150mg magnesium per 30g serving.
Source: Seifert B et al. Zentralbl Gynakol. 1989;111(11):755-760 -- magnesium supplementation reduced dysmenorrhea; Altura BM, Altura BT. Magnesium. 1985;4(5-6):175-194 -- magnesium and smooth muscle relaxation
🫖
Fresh ginger
Ginger contains gingerols and shogaols, compounds with anti-inflammatory and prostaglandin-inhibiting properties. One small RCT found ginger may be as effective as ibuprofen for period pain when taken from day 1 of menstruation.
Source: Ozgoli G et al. J Midwifery Womens Health. 2009;54(2):74-78 (n=150 women, ginger 250mg 4x/day vs ibuprofen 400mg 4x/day; no significant difference in pain relief at day 3)
🧄
Walnuts and flaxseed
Plant-based sources of ALA omega-3s. While less potent than EPA/DHA from fish, regular consumption supports an anti-inflammatory dietary pattern. Add to oats, yogurt, or salads.
Source: Calder PC. Biochim Biophys Acta. 2011;1812(7):791-800 -- omega-3 fatty acids and inflammation pathways
Minimize during menstruation
Trans fats and refined vegetable oils (may increase pro-inflammatory prostaglandins) -- red meat in excess -- alcohol (may increase prostaglandin activity) -- excess caffeine (may increase uterine tension in some women; individual responses vary)
🪧
Bloating
Luteal-phase GI changes
🧀
Probiotic foods (yogurt, kefir, kimchi)
Progesterone slows gut motility in the luteal phase, which can cause bloating and constipation. Probiotic-rich foods support gut microbiome balance and may help maintain regularity. Look for live active cultures on the label.
Source: Wald A et al. Gastroenterology. 1981;80(6):1497-1500 -- progesterone slows gastrointestinal transit; Bharadwaj S et al. J Clin Gastroenterol. 2015;49(4):270-277 -- sex hormones and GI motility
🥬
Cooked vegetables over raw
When gut motility is slower in the luteal phase, cooked vegetables (zucchini, carrots, spinach) are easier to digest than raw cruciferous veg. Steaming or roasting breaks down fiber and reduces gas production without removing nutrients.
Source: Guidance consistent with USDA Dietary Guidelines 2020-2025 and Health Canada; applied here in context of Wald et al. 1981 GI motility findings
🏵
Fennel (seeds or bulb)
Fennel has a long history as a carminative (gas-reducing) herb. Animal and limited human studies suggest fennel may relax intestinal muscles and reduce bloating. Brew as tea or slice raw into salads.
Source: Alexandrovich I et al. Altern Ther Health Med. 2003;9(4):58-61 -- fennel seed oil reduced intestinal spasm; evidence in women specifically is limited; individual results may vary
💧
Increase water intake
Counterintuitively, adequate hydration reduces water retention rather than causing it. Aim for at least 2L daily. Herbal teas (peppermint, ginger) may also support digestion.
Source: Dietary fluid recommendations consistent with Health Canada Dietary Reference Intakes; USDA Dietary Guidelines 2020-2025
Minimize to reduce bloating
High-sodium processed foods (drive water retention) -- carbonated drinks -- alcohol (disrupts GI function and worsens water retention) -- large quantities of raw brassicas (broccoli, cabbage, Brussels sprouts) if you are sensitive -- sugar alcohols (sorbitol, mannitol in sugar-free products)
🧠
Brain fog
Focus, concentration and energy
🥩
Iron-rich foods (red meat, lentils, spinach)
Iron loss during menstruation can reduce cognitive performance and increase fatigue. Even mild iron depletion without clinical anaemia may impair attention and working memory in women of reproductive age. Pairing plant iron sources with vitamin C increases absorption.
Source: Angeli A et al. Eur J Sport Sci. 2016 -- iron loss during menstruation impacts exercise performance and energy; Bruner AB et al. Lancet. 1996;348(9033):992-996 -- iron repletion improved attention and learning in non-anaemic iron-deficient adolescent girls
🧠
Oily fish and walnuts (DHA omega-3)
DHA is a structural component of brain cell membranes. Adequate omega-3 intake is associated with better cognitive function and mood regulation. Research suggests DHA may support working memory and reduce inflammatory signalling in the brain.
Source: Yurko-Mauro K et al. Alzheimers Dement. 2010;6(6):456-464 -- DHA supplementation improved memory; Calder PC. Biochim Biophys Acta. 2011;1812(7):791-800 -- omega-3 and neuro-inflammation
🍠
Complex carbohydrates (oats, sweet potato, brown rice)
The brain runs on glucose. Slow-releasing complex carbohydrates maintain stable blood glucose throughout the day, avoiding the energy crashes that worsen brain fog. Avoid skipping meals, especially during menstruation when energy demand may be higher.
Source: Consistent with USDA Dietary Guidelines 2020-2025 and Health Canada guidelines for carbohydrate quality; blood glucose and cognitive function evidence: Nilsson A et al. Appetite. 2012;58(1):200-207
🥚
Eggs and dark leafy greens (choline, folate)
Choline (eggs, liver) is an essential nutrient for acetylcholine synthesis, a neurotransmitter involved in attention and memory. Folate (leafy greens, legumes) supports neural methylation and may reduce fatigue-related cognitive slowing.
Source: Poly C et al. Am J Clin Nutr. 2011;94(6):1584-1591 -- choline and cognitive performance; Penland JG. Magnes Res. 1995 -- B-vitamin status and cognition in women
Minimize for better focus
Ultra-processed foods and refined sugar (blood glucose spikes cause energy crashes) -- excessive alcohol (disrupts sleep quality, worsening next-day cognition) -- skipping meals (allows blood glucose to drop)
💕
PMS and mood
Late luteal phase support
🧅
Dairy, fortified foods and sunlight (calcium and vitamin D)
Calcium is the best-studied dietary intervention for PMS. In the largest RCT to date, 1200mg/day calcium carbonate over three menstrual cycles reduced total PMS symptom scores by 48% compared to placebo. Vitamin D is required for calcium absorption and may independently reduce PMS risk. Aim for calcium-rich foods daily: yogurt, milk, fortified plant milks, hard cheese, sardines with bones.
Source: Thys-Jacobs S et al. Am J Obstet Gynecol. 1998;179(2):444-452 (n=466 women, multicentre RCT, calcium significantly reduced PMS symptoms at cycle 3); Bertone-Johnson ER et al. Am J Clin Nutr. 2005;81(4):971-978 (n=3025 women, high calcium and vitamin D intake associated with 40% lower PMS risk)
🌰
Magnesium-rich foods (pumpkin seeds, almonds, dark chocolate, legumes)
Magnesium may reduce anxiety, irritability, and fluid retention associated with PMS. Several small studies suggest magnesium is lower in women with PMS compared to symptom-free controls. Dietary magnesium may not be sufficient alone; food sources are worth prioritizing regardless.
Source: Facchinetti F et al. Obstet Gynecol. 1991;78(2):177-181 (n=32 women, magnesium oxide reduced late luteal phase dysphoric symptoms vs placebo); De Souza MC et al. J Womens Health Gend Based Med. 2000;9(2):131-139 -- magnesium and PMS anxiety
🍗
Vitamin B6 (chicken, turkey, fish, banana, potatoes)
B6 (pyridoxine) is a cofactor in serotonin and dopamine synthesis. A systematic review of nine trials found B6 may be twice as effective as placebo for PMS mood symptoms. Dietary sources are preferable to high-dose supplementation, which can cause nerve damage in excess.
Source: Wyatt KM et al. BMJ. 1999;318(7195):1375-1381 (systematic review and meta-analysis, 9 RCTs, n=940 women; B6 up to 100mg/day appeared beneficial for mood symptoms of PMS)
🍚
Complex carbohydrates throughout the day
Carbohydrate intake supports tryptophan uptake into the brain, where it converts to serotonin. Research suggests women with PMS may eat more carbohydrates in the luteal phase as a natural serotonin-seeking behaviour. Prioritizing complex carbs (not refined sugar) over this period may stabilize mood.
Source: Fernstrom JD. J Nutr. 1994;124(8 Suppl):1503S-1508S -- carbohydrate intake and serotonin synthesis; Wurtman JJ et al. Obes Res. 1995;3 Suppl 4:477S-480S -- carbohydrate craving and mood in PMS
Minimize in late luteal phase
Alcohol (disrupts GABA and serotonin signalling, worsens mood instability and sleep) -- excess caffeine (may worsen anxiety and breast tenderness; individual responses vary) -- refined sugar (blood glucose spikes and crashes amplify mood swings) -- high-sodium foods (worsen fluid retention and bloating)
🥊
Fatigue and low energy
Especially during and after menstruation
🥩
Red meat, liver, dark poultry (haem iron)
Haem iron from animal sources is absorbed at 15 to 35%, significantly higher than non-haem iron from plants (2 to 20%). Women lose 30 to 80ml of blood per cycle on average, making iron replenishment an important priority during and after menstruation.
Source: Angeli A et al. Eur J Sport Sci. 2016 -- iron loss during menstruation; Hallberg L et al. Scand J Gastroenterol. 1979;14(7):769-779 -- haem vs non-haem iron absorption rates
🌿
Lentils, spinach, tofu with vitamin C
Plant-based iron paired with vitamin C (bell pepper, citrus, kiwi) can increase non-haem absorption by up to 6-fold. This combination is especially important for women who eat little or no meat. Black tea, coffee, and calcium consumed at the same meal reduce iron absorption.
Source: Hallberg L et al. Am J Clin Nutr. 1989;49(1):140-144 -- ascorbic acid and iron absorption; Cook JD, Reddy MB. Am J Clin Nutr. 2001;73(1):93-98 -- enhancers and inhibitors of non-haem iron absorption
🍳
B12 (eggs, fish, dairy, fortified foods)
B12 deficiency is a common and under-recognized cause of fatigue in women, particularly those following plant-heavy diets. B12 is required for red blood cell formation. Deficiency mimics iron-deficiency fatigue and often goes undetected unless specifically tested.
Source: Health Canada dietary reference intakes for B12; Allen LH. Am J Clin Nutr. 2008;89(2):693S-696S -- B12 deficiency epidemiology and consequences
Avoid at the same meal as iron-rich foods
Black tea and coffee (polyphenols inhibit iron absorption by up to 60%) -- dairy in large quantities at the same meal (calcium competes with iron absorption) -- take these 1 to 2 hours before or after iron-rich meals where possible
